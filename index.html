Cane Tempo Trainer

T he aim of our project is to create a proof-of-concept for a tool that can help blind and/or visually impaired (B/VI) individuals and orientation and mobility (O&M) instructors in their training. We are inspired by the Cane Game developed by Prof. Paul Ruvolo and the OCCaM Lab at Olin College [1].

Why it matters.

W e chose to focus on the importance of learning how to move the cane at a consistent rate. The speed should somewhat approximate that which will be reached during ambulation [2], and the cane user should walk out-of-step with their cane sweeping motion such that their forward foot is on the opposite side of their body as the cane tip [3]. The first step to this training is practicing sweeping the cane without moving, and the early stages of learning should be geared for accuracy and not speed [2].

Gathering Data

We gathered data using the MATLAB mobile app to record accelerometer data. With a phone attached to the back of the hand, we gathered data to refect the movement of the cane. We collected both "clean" and "noisy" data by going at a consistent and inconsistent frequency.

Data Analysis

U sing Fourier transforms, we analyzed the data to reconstruct the tempo of sweeping the cane back and forth. With our algorithm, O&M instructors and their students can get an empirical measure of how consistent their rate of sweeping is.

Results

We found that clean data resulted in a pair of peak frequencies in the y and z-axes. This means that the frequency of the cane sweep is consistent, which is the goal when learning how to properly navigate with a cane.

Impact

B ecause practice is important for mastery of a skill, we hope that this proof-of-concept can be used to help B/VI individuals in the early stages of learning how to use a white cane. Students can get objective, quantified feedback about the performance of their sweeps without the direct instruction of an O&M. Furthermore, O&M instructors may be able to use our tool to remotely help their students and get results without having face-to-face, active lesson time.
